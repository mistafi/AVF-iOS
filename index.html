Week 2 Research

'Live' data resources

Less obvious perks of using 'live' data resources include logins and payments. How could Twitter be used in these two scenarios?

In terms of perks, using Twitter as a login could make it easier for the user to create a new account and prevent users from having to learn another password. You might also use Twitter to create a single sign-on account for your app that could include multiple payment methods.

My major concern with that would be the method by which you would have to pass the credentials securely, and how that would access payment methods, e.g. credit cards and checking accounts, in a secure way.

Search for JSON-P data sources

Visit Programmableweb.com and search for JSON-P data sources. Why would we need JSON-P, specifically?

JSON-P allows you to get data across domains, and since it pulls JavaScript instead of JSON you do not need a JSON parser. However, due to security concerns, many look to CORS, which is newer and regarded to be a better method for getting data across domains.

Based on the JSON-P search results: what data resource(s) do you think might pair well with which types of Native device features?

  • GPS - Altergeo, Bing maps, Bongo, Cloudmade, Flickr, Garmin
  • Camera - Blipfoto, Brightcove, Camera Plus, Instagram, Flickr
  • Accelerometer - Bongo, Bing maps, Citybikes, Garmin

Functionality Intentions

Please describe any design and functionality intentions you may have for your Demo app components this month.

I intend on pairing the camera with Instagram so a user can upload their own photos, and pairing Yelp and Twitter with the GPS to pull items closest to the user's location.

Lessons Learned

Briefly describe some past 'lessons learned' regarding workflow as it relates to research, design, development, etc.

One of the things I've learned is to dream first. Dream and draw, sketching your ideas on paper and making notes. Next, start thinking about how the app or site should function, and pull those ideas into app or site pages. Follow up your sketches with research on how you can accomplish what you want to do. Then, go back and revise, research some more, then revise. After you have condensed your ideas, create some mock-ups with HTML. This gives you a reusable code-base to work from.

After you have tweaked your HTML as far as you can without building the app or site, then it's time for development to start. Be patient with your development because, inevitably, not everything will work as expected the first time around. There will even be many times where you will have to go back and research methods, or continue to sketch and mock-up ideas before you get it just right.

Try to keep everything in perspective, and have an understanding of what you can accomplish in the time given. Remember, everything you create can be completed in iterations. You don't need to have the best, fastest, most complete or brightest the first time around. Separate your app or site's main functionality from what would be extra features, and add those on in steps, or iterations. Always stay positive, and keep your eye on the prize.

Week 3 Research

Accessibility

What are some of the ways that you could change your HTML on your Demo app to be more accessible? (Remember, you can use the Section tag with jQuery, for instance)

After looking over my HTML, I could not find any areas I could improve since I had accessibility in mind from the outset. I do my best not to use a lot of images, and instead use CSS for backgrounds and the like. All of the identifying images for the Features and API areas are not images at all, but rather are a webfont. The only image I have in the site is presentational, coded in CSS, and the associated text is not embedded in the image, but is text on the page instead.

What I will continue to do in the future is use proper sections and identifying headers, and any images that appear in content will have alt text.

Accessibility Frustrations

Visit a video service on the web (like YouTube) and search for "Screen Reader Demo" and "Mobile Screen Reader" - what are some of the common frustrations (or common support mechanisms) that you see among the examples that are given - how could you fix them (or use them)?

The most common issue seems to be that screen readers are not easy to use to begin with, and require a lot of training. Beyond that, identifying major sections and accessing links quickly are a huge issue.

It seems the best way to fix them would be to simplify the content to the point where everything is grouped in logical sections under major headers on the page, and clearly identifying and choosing succinct link text to make it easier to make logical assumptions for the impaired user.
